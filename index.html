About the RES Tool

What is the Researcher Embeddedness Statement?

The Researcher Embeddedness Statement (RES) is a comprehensive self-assessment tool designed to help researchers evaluate and articulate their positioning in relation to their research community and context. Embeddedness refers to the degree to which a researcher has direct connections, experience, and relationships with the community, country, or group they are studying.

Why is Researcher Embeddedness Important?

  • Research Validity: Understanding your positionality helps ensure more rigorous and transparent research
  • Ethical Research: Awareness of your positioning enables more ethical engagement with communities
  • Methodological Transparency: Explicitly discussing embeddedness strengthens your methodology section
  • Reflexivity: The tool encourages critical reflection on how your relationship to the community influences interpretation

How to Use This Tool

  1. Start the Assessment: Click "Start Assessment" to begin the 26-question questionnaire
  2. Answer Questions: Respond honestly to questions about your relationship to the research context
  3. Review Results: Receive a personalized profile and recommendations for your methodology discussion
  4. Use in Your Research: Incorporate the suggested questions and findings into your manuscript

Assessment Dimensions

The tool evaluates embeddedness across eight key dimensions:

  • Country/Society Relationship
  • Community Connection
  • Language Proficiency
  • Research Experience
  • Field Presence
  • Community Collaboration
  • Positionality Awareness
  • Data & Methodological Constraints

Embeddedness Profiles

Based on your responses, you will receive one of five embeddedness profiles:

  • Highly Embedded: Strong connections and deep engagement with the research community
  • Moderately Embedded: Balanced insider and outsider perspectives
  • Minimally Embedded: Limited direct connections but some community engagement
  • Weakly Embedded: Primarily external positioning with minimal community relationships
